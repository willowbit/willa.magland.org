title: "Reflecting on a Year of Journaling"
date: 2025-09-25
---

In August I reached the last pages of my journal, which I had started almost the same day last year. This was a big achievement for me. I'd never completed a journal before, and it had been my first year of consistently keeping one.

![An animated GIF of scans of my journal](/img/journal.gif)

It was a [TRU RED softcover journal from Staples](https://www.staples.com/tru-red-5-5-x-8-medium-flexible-cover-narrow-ruled-journal-cognac-tr61952/product_24563057), and it served me well. Now I am using the Leuchtturm1917 paperback, which is a bit smaller and a better fit for me.

My basic journaling setup is very simple. On a new day I skip a line and write the date. What usually follows is a checklist, a quick description of what I dreamed (if I remember), quick observations, and whatever else is on my mind.

Throughout the day I'll note down things I want to remember, such as tasks, bits of wisdom, songs and movie titles, or a friend's favorite ice cream flavor.

These notes are rarely made in full sentences. They are scribbled down as fast as possible, cramped up next to each other. I skip lines when I feel like it. If there's some unused space on the right side next to a list, it gets filled in with a bored doodle. I'll throw bits of ephemera in there too, when I feel like it.

I write in ballpoint pens, which I cram between the pages and secure with the elastic strap. The size of a journal is very important for me. With the Leuchtturm, I've settled on the B6 size, which is smaller than the A5 but not quite the A6. I may be able to go smaller, but I'm not sure.

![A picture of my brown-cover journal in the sunlit grass.](/img/journal.jpg)

I don't look back at old entries too often, but when I do it's mostly just for fun. The details of the past become incomprehensible without context, which makes them harden into a kind of poetry. Occasionally I'll want to remember the name of something and I'll go back into my journal to find it.

My advice to new journalers would be the following:
1. Bring it everywhere: your journal is a part of you.
2. Don't baby it: write in the rain, throw it on the ground, toss it at the bottom of your bag, rip its pages and crack the spine. The internet will try to convince you that journaling is "aesthetic." It's not. It is ugly and messy and beautiful.
3. Don't search for the perfect system: it doesn't exist. I highly recommend a simple day-by-day entry system. It doesn't need to be complicated.
4. Ignore everything I've said: your journal is yours, and its up to you what you want it to be.

Hope this helps,

Willa

_P.S. Also, don't forget to put your email and phone number on the inside cover!_
