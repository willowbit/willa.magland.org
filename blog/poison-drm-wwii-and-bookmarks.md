Many things happened this week, and many of them were beautiful. My general dislike for cities was once again challenged, this time by Chicago's brilliant skyline and immersive culture. I also found quite a few beautiful things on the internet...

1.  With brutal honesty, Adam Mastroianna writes about what it's like **[Having a Skull full of Poison](https://www.experimental-history.com/p/its-very-weird-to-have-a-skull-full)**, his preferred term for depression and mental illness. Mastroianna shares his experience and discusses how changing our perspectives allows us to learn to live *with* our mental illness, rather than *without.*

2.  Uncover [**The Hotel Majestic**](https://hotelmajesticphilly.blogspot.com/?m=1)'s lost history through old postcards, letters, and newspaper clippings.

3.  Enjoy the Wordle, but need something a little different? **[Ridella](https://ridella.xyz/)** offers a daily riddle. But don't worry, they're fairly easy... so far.

4.  Each of us has had an experience where [Internet Archive's Wayback Machine](https://web.archive.org/) has undeniably saved our ass. Personally, the resource has proved itself vital for learning about the things I love, allowing me to explore the [carefully hand-crafted fan sites](https://web.archive.org/web/20050102090402/http://www.kdge.com/houseofleaves4.html) of the early two-thousands (notably, the [Crazy Psychos)](https://web.archive.org/web/20060216232529/http://poe.org/). Just a few minutes exploring the site's archives feels immersive, and more importantly, *real. *This is why Tom Scocca suggests **[Making the Wayback Machine the Real Internet](https://indignity.substack.com/p/make-the-wayback-machine-the-real)**, when the web of the present is becoming all the more [generative and un-intimate](https://www.forbes.com/sites/bernardmarr/2023/08/14/is-generative-ai-destroying-the-internet/).

5.  **[The Boiling Frog of Digital Freedom.](https://gazoche.xyz/posts/boiling-frog/)**

6.  **[Oakland Library's "Found in a Library Book"](https://oaklandlibrary.org/found-in-a-library-book/)**: What do you use as a bookmark, besides, well, an actual *bookmark*?

7.  Nina Siegal writes on **[The Roma Holocaust](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/08/29/arts/roma-database-holocaust.html?unlocked_article_code=83xxEFptsOy7IodD4fs6FwTVQfsTqZYvwZfI_WwFXuQTIW57VBskXMF5GJujtD-84vGfg30dtP1zz_wAmUxrZVcSviYDVbGpPjWCgWqrIFZI1tbCSdtc-qoMxYOTjkd3h2Yo_kJFSlDr92zfzKg4_ShL0rftjZc8IwNdFafkqTkgB0Hq0nxKaQzVUOdYtyzYT2aijb3YGH7NG0zvR6qc9fJcH26kcOJRJCdJc9wteEIj0pG21fCsEMEJod_zNcBZiLNdRAIuxQhZrGfPnRuTykDm6cdgaX-8Hsyt9IXQhls3i1RDwzbHHHlWn2P_4RUa5BtwFEsr1p_dw4HkdLc_&smid=url-share)**, an all too often forgotten aspect of the second world war. The article reminded me of Berlin's [Memorial to the Sinti and Roma Victims](https://www.bbc.com/news/av/world-europe-20071308), which I was able to visit in spring.

8.  I've found that nothing pleases me more than reading about people's grandparents. I'm very lucky to have all four of my grandparents around, and **[this post by Cat Hicks on craft](https://www.drcathicks.com/post/on-craft)** deeply reminded me of my brilliant grandfather, who does carpentry in his free time.
